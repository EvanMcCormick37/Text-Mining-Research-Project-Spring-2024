Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA)¶

Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA for short) is a method of organizing text documents based on topics. The premise is that rather than looking at each document as simply a collection of terms, we add an intermediate object - a topic. Topics are collections of terms, each assigned a certain probability. Documents can then be thought of as collections of topics, each assigned a certain probability. For example, a news article about Qatar building new stadiums in preparation for the 2022 World Cup might be 30% infrastructure, 20% business, and 30% sports and 20% politics.

In LDA, the topics are assigned algorithmically and randomly, so we don't know what they are before-hand. But when you look at the word distributions within the topics, they're often quite clear.

For this LDA analysis, I'll be using the text from the WorldNewsAPI datasource.

Thoughts¶

Some of these 'topics' do correspond to clear topics present in different articles. Here's what I would label them, in order:

1. Primary School and Students
2. The LGBT Community and the Transgender Rights Movement
3. State Laws and Bills
4. ???
5. The Brianna Ghey case
6. Transgender women in sports

The most confusing topic is number 4. There are no clear words relating to the transgender issue. Frankly this could be considered a 'stop-topic', corresponding to the language that most news articles use surrounding their stories. It seems like many common words have all been grouped into this one topic. I bet if we looked at topic distributions, this topic would be reasonably present in all articles combed through. It's also interesting that the Brianna Ghey case shows up as a topic here. Normally, topics are supposed to be distributed throughout documents, but I'm guessing that the stories on Brianna Ghey were so numerous, and so distinct in their language, that the model felt they deserved their own separate topic.
